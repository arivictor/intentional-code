Architecture happens the moment your code stops being trivial. Most developers stumble into architecture by accident — one day you're writing a script, and the next you're responsible for a growing system with expectations, users, and consequences. Along the way you made decisions about how to organise code, where logic belongs, and how different parts of the system interact. Without a clear decision framework, the codebase slowly becomes tangled and hard to change.

The gap between uncoordinated code and well-architected projects can feel impossibly wide. The structure made sense to someone, just not to you. Why this separation and not another? What were the developers seeing or asking that you weren't?

This is a reference to the patterns and principles that help you answer three questions: how should I structure this codebase? How do I model complex business logic? How do I separate technical detail from core logic? Each page stands alone — focused, with idiomatic Go examples. Jump to what you need; come back when you need it again.
